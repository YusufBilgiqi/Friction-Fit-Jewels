Background · Section 01

History & Development

Friction jeweling began in Switzerland around 1920 but did not see significant use in the United States until the early 1930s. The technique required solving two distinct engineering problems before it could become practical.

PROBLEMS
Two Problems That Had to Be Solved
Problem 1 — Jewel Strength
Pressing a jewel into a plate with the force required for a reliable friction fit risked breaking the jewel. Natural ruby and sapphire, with their internal flaws, could not reliably withstand this pressure. The solution was the development of synthetic ruby and sapphire — far more uniform and stronger than natural material. Without synthetic corundum, friction jeweling at scale was impossible.
Problem 2 — Hole Centring
Early friction jewels did not always have their pivot holes perfectly centred. Some American watch companies solved this by first pressing the jewel into a brass setting, then — while the jewel and its hole were running true in a lathe — turning the outside diameter of the setting to size. This centred the hole in the setting. The setting was then frictioned into the plate.

Later improvements in jewel manufacturing made it possible to friction jewels directly into the plate with the hole reliably on centre — eliminating the need for intermediate settings.
Why friction jeweling mattered. Before friction jewels, jewels set in bezels made adjusting end shake impractical in many movements. Friction jewels could be pressed to a precise depth, enabling proper end shake setting during assembly — a significant improvement in both serviceability and performance.
Background · Section 02

Buying Vintage Sets

Choosing the right jeweling tool depends on what era of movements you work on. Condition when buying vintage is critical — some components can be replaced, others cannot.

Post-1930 Movements (Friction Jewels)
A Horia-style clone jewel press will work adequately on a budget. These cover the needs of friction-jeweled movements from the modern era and are more affordable than a full Seitz.
Pre-1930 Movements (Rubbed-In Jewels)
A Seitz press is needed. Seitz sets were designed specifically to work on the older movements with rubbed-in jewels and jewel settings — the Horia-style tool is not adequate for this work.
Seitz Alternatives
Other brands exist — Favorite, C&E Marshall, K&D — but Seitz was the original and remains the standard. Parts availability and quality consistency are better for Seitz.
CONDITION
What Can Be Replaced, What Cannot
Can Be Replaced New
Pushers that cannot be redressed can still be purchased new for Seitz presses.

Reamers that are damaged cannot usually be resharpened, but can also be purchased new.
Cannot Easily Be Replaced
The frame itself — damage to the frame or the micrometer mechanism is serious. Many vintage accessories can be bought separately on eBay, but the frame is the foundation.
Ask lots of questions when buying vintage. Try to buy as complete a set as possible. An incomplete set is not necessarily a deal-breaker if the missing accessories are available separately — but verify before committing. Condition of pushers and reamers should be specifically asked about.
Seitz Tooling · Section 03

The Frame & Reamers

The Seitz frame is similar to a staking set but has a lever handle used to press down on the pusher holder. The reamers are used to bring the plate hole to the exact size required for the incoming jewel.

REAMERS
Size Range and Relationship to Jewels

Reamers are graduated in size and are 0.01mm smaller than the jewel they are designed to prepare a hole for. This 0.01mm undersize creates the interference fit — the jewel is slightly larger than the hole, producing the friction that holds it securely.

TECHNIQUE
Correct Reaming Technique
→
Always Turn Clockwise — Continuously
Never use a back-and-forth turning motion with the reamer. Always turn in a continuous clockwise direction. Back-and-forth motion causes the hole to go out of round and off-centre — the exact opposite of what is needed.
→
Never Lock or Hold the Plate Tight
Never hold the plate or bridge rigidly fixed while reaming. The plate must be free to move slightly — locking it causes the reamer to cut off-centre as the tool exerts sideways force. Let the plate find its own position on the anvil.
→
Using the Reamer as a Plunge Gauge
If the old jewel comes out in pieces and cannot be measured directly, the reamer can be used to determine the hole size. Insert a reamer and check whether it passes the cutting surface and into the burnishing section. A reamer that is the right size for a hole will insert past the cutter to the burnishing section. If it stops at the cutter, drop down one size and try again.
The reamer has two sections — a cutting surface and, above it, a burnishing surface. The burnishing surface is slightly smaller than the cutter and polishes the hole after cutting. The jewel size that fits a correctly reamed hole corresponds to the reamer's designation plus 0.01mm (i.e. the jewel is 0.01mm larger than the reamer).

TYPES
Flat, Concave, and Pump
Pusher TypeFace ProfileUsed ForKey Reason
Flat Pusher Flat face Train jewels — pressing in and pushing out. Also used as an anvil when inverted. Train jewels have flat faces; the flat pusher contacts the full jewel surface evenly.
Concave Pusher Concave (dished) face Pressing in convex balance hole jewels. Pump Pusher Concave face with a small centre pin ("pump centre") Train or balance hole jewels — primarily for production use. The pump centre drops into the jewel's pivot hole and self-centres the pusher on the jewel without manual alignment. Faster for large quantities; also protects the hole edge from damage caused by an off-centre flat pusher.
Seitz Tooling · Section 05

Anvils, Stumps & Stakes

Seitz calls these "stakes" — not to be confused with the stakes in a staking set. They support the plate or bridge during reaming or jewel pressing, and their selection affects both accuracy and the risk of distortion.

Some anvils are solid; others have graduated holes through them. The hole in the anvil must be large enough for the jewel to pass through as it is pressed out — if the hole is too small the jewel will not clear and the plate will lift off the anvil before the jewel is free.

Solid Anvils
Used when pressing jewels in — the plate rests flat on the solid surface while the pusher presses the jewel into the hole from above. Also used for reaming operations.
Holed Anvils
Used when pressing jewels out — the hole allows the ejected jewel to fall through rather than catching between the jewel and the plate. The hole must be sized to clear the jewel.
Platform Stake (Table)
The large flat platform — known as the "table" — is the main working surface. It is removed when smaller anvils need to be used in its place. The platform stake also serves as the mounting point for uprighting pins when installed.
⚠
The anvil must support the plate around the jewel hole. If the plate is not sitting flat — if there is a recess around the jewel hole that is not supported — the plate may bend or distort as the jewel is pushed out or in. Select an anvil whose diameter fits into any recess so the plate sits flat and fully supported.
Seitz Tooling · Section 06

Hole Closers & Burr Removal

Two finishing operations are needed after reaming: closing an oversized hole, and removing the burr left by the reamer around the hole edge.

HOLE CLOSERS
Correcting a Slightly Oversized Hole

If a plate hole has been reamed slightly too large for the available jewel, a hole closer can be used to reduce it. The hole closer fits into the pusher holder and is used to close the hole by a maximum of 0.01–0.02mm only. This is a minor correction — not a method for substantially reducing an oversized hole. Beyond this range, the plate material must be replaced or a larger jewel selected.

BURR
Three Ways to Remove the Reaming Burr

The reaming process leaves a small burr of metal around the hole edge. This burr must be removed before pressing in the jewel — otherwise it will cause the jewel to sit proud or at an angle.

1
Dedicated Burr Removing Tool
Some Seitz sets include a purpose-made burr remover. Many vintage examples are in poor condition and would need to be dressed or replaced. New ones can be purchased from watch material suppliers.
2
Wheel Countersink (Preferred)
A wheel countersink (such as the Bergeon 1897) is one of the best alternatives. Available from watch material suppliers in sets of six. Some are double-ended: one disc has a sharp edge for cutting the countersink; the other has a rounded edge for burnishing and polishing the sink. Applied lightly around the hole edge until the burr is cleanly removed.
3
Round Dental Burr
A round dental burr mounted in a pin vise works well as an improvised burr remover. Applied very lightly — only enough to remove the raised edge without cutting a significant countersink into the plate.
↑
In a Pinch — Oversized Reamer Bevel
If nothing else is available, the bevelled point of a reamer about two sizes larger than the hole can be used. Apply very lightly — the bevel will remove the burr without cutting the plate if used with care.

Seitz Tooling · Section 08

Special-Purpose Accessories

Several accessories serve very specific functions beyond standard jewel work. Some are indispensable; others are nice-to-haves with readily available alternatives.

→
Setting Lever Pushers
One stake and two pointed pushers. Used to adjust the pallet fork up or down when correcting clearance between the pallet jewels and the escape wheel. A specialised but genuinely useful tool for pallet geometry correction.
→
Lanterning Tools
One stump and one pusher. Designed to tighten loose cannon pinions — but equally useful for tightening the pipes on second hands. The Seitz press's micrometer control allows adjustments under 1/100th of a mm, making it far more controlled than any hand method.
→
Hour Hand Tightening Pushers
Four sizes of pushers for closing hour hand pipes. The precision of the Seitz press makes these very valuable — small, repeatable adjustments to hand pipe diameter that would be difficult to achieve consistently with other methods.
→
Knurled Knob
Screws onto the micrometer spindle, converting the Seitz lever-press action into a screw-controlled downward movement — similar to the Horia style tool. Gives greater feel and control over pressing depth, particularly useful for delicate operations.
→
Spindle with Collets
Holds cutting broaches, smoothing broaches, and micro drill bits. Useful but most staking sets offer something similar. Not worth overpaying for separately.
→
Pivot Straighteners
Included in premium sets for balance and train pivots. The source's assessment: a better name would be pivot breakers — "I have never seen them used successfully to straighten a pivot." Treat them as a curiosity rather than a reliable tool.
→
Collet Holder
Primarily for holding winding stems while cutting and fitting them for crowns. A regular pin vise is more than adequate for this task — useful if it comes with the set, but not worth seeking out separately.
UPRIGHTING
Full Uprighting Procedure

When a pivot hole in a bridge is worn oval but the opposing pivot hole in the mainplate is sound, uprighting restores the axis relationship before a new bushing is fitted.

1
Assemble Bridge onto Mainplate
Install the bridge onto the mainplate in its normal position. Both plates together in assembled relationship.
2
Place Good Hole Over the Spring-Loaded Pin
Install the correctly-sized uprighting pin into the press frame (with the table platform removed). Place the good, undamaged pivot hole over the spring-loaded pin. This pin now establishes the axis — it defines where the upper hole should be relative to the lower.
3
Ream the Damaged Hole
With the axis established by the pin below, ream the damaged upper hole to the correct size for a new bushing. The pin ensures the reamed hole is centred on the correct axis — not on the worn, off-centre original hole.
4
Press In and Ream the New Bushing
Press the new bushing into the enlarged hole. Ream the bushing's bore to fit the pivot, then broach if needed to achieve the correct clearance for the pivot or centre jewel hole.
Jewel Types · Section 09

Train Jewel Configurations

Friction fit train jewels come in only two hole configurations — and one additional classification for centre wheel jewels. The jewel's curved edge always goes into the hole first.

Straight-Sided with Curved Edge
All friction train jewels share this basic form — straight outer sides with a curved (radiused) edge on the oil-sink side. The curved edge goes into the plate hole first when pressing in. This design allows the jewel to be pressed smoothly without the edge catching.
Centre Wheel Jewels
Classified separately because of their large centre hole. The bore is much wider than a standard train jewel due to the larger diameter of the centre wheel arbor. Sizing and selection follows the same principles but the hole dimension is in a different range.
HOLE TYPES
Cylindrical vs. Olive (Olived) Hole
Cylindrical Hole Jewels
A plain straight bore through the jewel. Found in most train jewels across standard-grade movements. Available from 0.80mm to 3.0mm outside diameter.
Olive (Olived) Hole Jewels
The bore is convex inside — widest at the centre, narrowing at each face. Found in high-grade movements. Reduces contact area between pivot and jewel in vertical positions, equalising friction across watch orientations. Available from 0.70mm to 1.2mm outside diameter.

METHOD
How to Determine the Required Diameter
IF JEWEL COMES OUT INTACT
Measure the Old Jewel
Use a digital micrometer to measure the outside diameter of the removed jewel directly. Select a new jewel with the same diameter. This is the preferred method — most reliable.
IF JEWEL COMES OUT IN PIECES
Use the Reamer as a Gauge
Insert a reamer into the hole. If it passes the cutting surface into the burnishing section — this reamer's corresponding jewel size is correct. If it stops at the cutter — drop one size down and try again. Work down until the reamer slides past the cutter.
If the exact diameter is unavailable: Select a jewel one size larger than the original. Enlarge the plate hole with the appropriate reamer to fit the wider jewel. Always select the same diameter as the original if possible — enlarging the plate hole is a last resort, not a first choice.

Removal & Installation · Section 12

Removing Old Jewels

Always push from the correct side — and always support the plate properly. An unsupported plate will bend before the jewel releases.

1
Select Anvil with Clearance Hole
Choose a stump with a hole large enough for the jewel to fall through as it is pressed out. The stump must be the right diameter to fit into any recess around the jewel hole — so the plate sits flat on the stump, fully supported around the hole.
2
Select the Correct Pusher
Select a flat pusher or pump pusher that is slightly smaller than the jewel. Too large and the pusher will catch the plate around the jewel; too small and it may tip into the hole and damage the jewel seat.
3
Orient the Plate Correctly
Place the plate with the oil-sink side facing down on the stump. Push down on the flat side of the jewel. Exception: a balance hole jewel is pushed out from the inside of the plate (and pushed back in from the outside).
4
Centre the Pusher and Press
Bring the pusher down, centred on the jewel. Press smoothly — the jewel should release with moderate, even pressure and fall into the hole in the stump below.
⚠
If the plate is not fully supported around the hole, it will bend. This is a common and easily avoided mistake. Take the time to select an anvil that supports the plate flat — a bent plate is a far bigger problem than a stubborn jewel.
Removal & Installation · Section 13

Setting Depth & Micrometer

The Seitz micrometer is graduated in 0.01mm increments. Before removing the old jewel, the micrometer can be indexed to its exact depth — so the new jewel can be pressed to precisely the same position.

INDEXING
Recording the Old Jewel Depth Before Removal
1
Set Up Tool with Stump and Pusher
Select the stump and pusher that will be used for pressing in the new jewel. Install both fully in the tool — they must be fully seated before taking any measurement.
2
Lower Pusher onto Old Jewel
Place the plate with the old jewel in position on the stump. Turn the micrometer clockwise to lower the pusher until it just rests on the flat face of the old jewel. Read the micrometer and record this number.
3
Calculate Press-Out Depth
Remove the plate. Turn the micrometer clockwise one full revolution (= 1mm) past the recorded reading. If the reading was 0.03, turn one full turn and stop at 0.03 again — the pusher is now 1mm lower than before, enough to push out most jewels encountered in normal work.
4
Press In New Jewel to Same Depth
With the new jewel positioned in the hole and the micrometer returned to the original recorded measurement, press in the new jewel. The micrometer stop will halt the pusher at exactly the same depth as the original jewel sat.
Flush vs. below-flush. If the new jewel is to be pressed flush with the plate surface, the micrometer depth-stop is not needed — press until the jewel is level with the plate. Depth indexing is only required when the jewel must sit below flush — most critically for balance hole jewels, where the precise oil gap between the jewel and the endstone is set by depth.
Removal & Installation · Section 14

Pressing In Jewels

With the old jewel removed and the depth measurement recorded, pressing in the new jewel is straightforward — provided the pusher selection and orientation are correct.

1
Select Correct Pusher
For a jewel pressed below flush: select a flat or pump pusher slightly smaller than the jewel.
For a jewel pressed flush: select a flat or pump pusher slightly larger than the jewel — the larger pusher face helps distribute force over the entire surface and ensures the jewel sits level.
2
Dial In the Depth Setting
If pressing below flush, set the micrometer to the depth recorded before the old jewel was removed. If pressing flush, no depth stop is needed.
3
Position the Jewel
Place the jewel in the hole with the flat side facing up (oil-sink side down, into the plate). Place the plate on the stump. Bring the pusher down, centred on the jewel.
4
Press In Smoothly
Apply steady lever pressure. The jewel should press in smoothly and stop at the micrometer setting. Do not force beyond the stop.
Balance hole jewel direction: the balance hole jewel is pushed out from the inside of the plate and pressed in from the outside — opposite to most train jewels. Always confirm orientation before pressing.
Removal & Installation · Section 15

Balance Hole Jewel Depth

The balance hole jewel must be set to a precise depth below flush — not pressed flush with the plate. The gap between the jewel's top surface and the plate surface is the oil space for the endstone assembly.

Standard oil gap
0.02mm
Below flush — jewel top to plate surface
Note
Varies by calibre — always measure both the plate and the jewel depth to confirm.
MEASURING
How to Measure the 0.02mm Depth Accurately
1
Set Up the Tool
Select a stump to support the plate or balance cock. Place it in the frame. Select the smallest flat pusher in the set and place it in the pusher holder.
2
Measure the Plate Surface
Place the plate or balance cock on the stump. Bring the pusher down onto the plate surface beside the jewel — on the flat area where the endstone will sit. Back the micrometer stop up against the pusher holder head to index this position. Record the micrometer reading.
3
Measure the Jewel Top Surface
Move the plate on the stump so the pusher is now over the centre of the jewel. Slowly turn the micrometer stop until the pusher just touches the top of the jewel. Record this second reading.
4
Calculate the Depth
The difference between the two readings is how far below flush the jewel currently sits. If fitting a new jewel, press it in until this difference equals 0.02mm (or the calibre-specific specification). The micrometer stop is set accordingly to halt the pusher at the correct depth.
Why this matters. Too little gap: the endstone sits too close to the balance hole jewel — insufficient space for the oil ring to form, and the jewel may actually contact the endstone. Too much gap: the oil cannot bridge across to form a proper ring and will break away. The 0.02mm specification is not approximate — it is the designed oil space for the assembly.
